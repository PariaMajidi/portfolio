This case study is about Claim History portion

What is Claim History?

Claim history is where users can find and track their submitted claims

The goal for 2024?

How might we make it easier for members to find their submitted claims and track its progress ?

How Claim History redesign started?

The business made a new contract with a new client and promised to give a real time decision on the submitted claims. In this way, users can see how much they are going to be paid, if any, right after they submit their claims. They can also visit claim history and find out how much they will be paid even if their claim has not processed yet.

business wanted to add a new feature to the claim history experience. But is the current experience effective, and will this addition improve it or complicate it?

Addressing usability issues or pursuing feature growth?

  • The mobile team conducted two usability studies for Claim History and found areas that confuse users.
  • The question was, whether to address usability issues first OR to implement real time response for claim history and then improve UX.
  • We had multiple meetings with the product and technical teams to understand the cost of each.

Design Discovery and empathizing:

Mobile team usability study summary:

The method: Unmoderated UZ advanced UX study, Participants: 8, Audience: General population, Age: 25-55, Standard screener with users who have health benefits product, Launched: September 2, 2022

Old design

Summary of critical usability issues: (Defining)

Usability issue1:

Users unable to understand the difference between recent online and processed claims and why there is duplications.

“To be honest I’m a little confused about the difference between Processed claims and Recent online claims.”

“So, I found myself checking on both the tabs because I wasn’t sure which category it fell into” 

Usability issue2:

Showing Pending claims under processed claims confused users as their initial idea of the processed claims tab was only claims that are processed and paid would be there.

“So that’s interesting…I thought everything processed would be whatever that’s been paid, but I see that there is one still pending, I’m wondering wouldn’t that fall under recent online claims…”

Research Recommendations

  • Consider merging the processed and recent online claims pages because the users cannot understand the difference.
  • If keeping two tabs is required, they recommended the following:
    • Clarify the titles of the tabs, I.e., Users perceive processed claims as anything that has been processed and paid, but 'Under review' claims also show up in this tab.
    • Have the Processed claims tab house all claims that have been processed and paid out. Have the Recent online claims tab house all claims that have been submitted but are not yet processed.
    • Avoid overlap of showing the same claim on each tab.

The claim history page structure for categorizing claims based on their processing status didn’t align with user’s expectations.>

We decided to ideate different designs and see the amount of effort and then decide which design we should go forward to meet the deadline, satisfy the business and the customer and being user friendly

Ideating:

IDEA #1: MVP Design: Old design + New feature

1
Duplications of the claims are very confusing. To be paid OR Paid?
One claim will be displayed in both tabs after it gets processed until 2 months
2
Pending claims appearing in processed claims are still confusing.
Pending claims also appear in processed claims
This design will be very quick to implement but won’t have a good user experience.

IDEA #2: Interim Design : Revised old design + New feature

1
Clarify the titles:
“Recent online” has changed to “In progress”
2
Avoid duplications of the claims
Claims after they get processed will be removed from in progress/recent online tab
3
Pending under in progress
Pending claims should not be considered as Processed
This idea requires a bit more time to implement. While the UX is not perfect, it’s reasonably acceptable

IDEA #3: Target Design: New design + New feature

1
Merge all claims in one table
Recent online and In progress are merged. Ideally, more filtering options will be added to the top of the table.
Ideally, based on the mobile discovery we recommended to combine the tabs.
This idea is the ideal solution with the best possible UX, but it will take a significant amount of time to implement fully
Question is which of these ideas should we choose? MVP, Interim OR Target

Summary of the workshops with technical team:

  • Target state needs almost double the time compared to Interim state which doesn’t meet the deadlines
  • Interim state can meet the deadline only if they implement it with some limitations

What is Interim state with some limitations?

Clarify the titles: ✔️
“Recent online” has changed to “In progress”
Avoid overlap ✔️
Claims after they get processed will be removed from in progress/recent online tab
Pending under in progress ❌
Pending claims should not be considered as Processed
Given the deadline, they were unable to deliver the final item

Testing

Methodology: Moderated Usability Study / Combination of navigation and content feedback questions / Conducted on Microsoft Teams / Participants from Member Panel – people who have Canada Life workplace benefits and have submitted online health claims in the past 3 months / 10 testers/ Canadians between age 40-65 years

Summary of critical usability issues: (Defining)
1
We validated that the limitations in Interim state bring confusion for users. This means:
The Pending claims need to be displayed in in-progress tab.
“I don’t understand why pending is in processed claims”
2
We also learnt that users had concerns about meaning and position of deferred claim status.
Clarify meaning of deferred as it was confusing for users
“Deferred, I am not 100% sure maybe just when the system is down and will be processed later. Pending and maybe Deferred should be under in progress”
3
Merge the two tabs into one table or consider moving processed and in progress tab closer to the table, to make it easier for users toggle between tabs.
4
Consider providing sorting option for the submitted claims to make it easier for users to look for previous claims.
5
Showing $0 “paid” and “to be paid” under processed and in progress tabs confused users Change the Paid status to something else when the amount is $0

This test once again confirmed the limitations of the interim state, highlighting critical usability issues. It reinforces that implementing the target design is the ideal solution

Design Delivery on Figma

The impact:

Call Centre:

The phone calls to the call centre regarding the claim history decreased 32% over the 3 months

Qualitrics

Not satisfied comments decreased 37% and satisfied comments increased 7%.

Next steps and my learnings:

I learned that, in reality, we cannot always design and implement the ideal situation for users. We may need to focus on a design that is more effective in the long term, allowing for future improvements and iterations. While there may be some limitations in the initial stage, we recognize that this foundation will ultimately lead us to the ideal design we are striving for.

For next steps, the team will remove the Interim state limitations and eventually will merge all the tables and implement the Target design.
